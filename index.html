Microcontroller Specifications

At the center of our architecture is the Arduino board. This device serves as the robot‚Äôs "Central Nervous System." It is an open-source electronics platform based on flexible, easy-to-use hardware and software. Its primary role in our project is to act as the master controller that bridges the gap between digital logic and physical movement.

The Arduino executes a high-frequency polling loop. üëÄ HC-SR04 Ultrasonic Integration

Sensory Perception Modules

The "Eyes" of the Team BETA robot are powered by the HC-SR04 Ultrasonic Sensor. This module works on the principle of echolocation, similar to the biological sonar used by bats and dolphins. It consists of two primary transducers: a transmitter that sends out an ultrasonic pulse (40kHz) and a receiver that waits for that pulse to bounce off an object and return.

By measuring the exact time of travel for these sound waves, the Arduino can determine the location of the user with high accuracy. This allows the robot to "see" the distance between itself and the human subject, providing the necessary data for the follow-logic to execute.

üì° The Physics of Sonar Waves

Time-of-Flight (ToF) Calculations

To calculate the distance to an object, we use the speed of sound as a constant. Sound travels through the air at approximately 343 meters per second. The Arduino measures the "Duration" of the pulse in microseconds. We then apply a mathematical formula to convert this time into physical distance (centimeters).

Formula: Distance = (Time √ó 0.034) / 2. We must divide by two because the sound wave travels a round-trip: from the robot to the target and back again. Without this calculation, the robot would perceive the target as being twice as far away as it actually is.

‚öôÔ∏è Autonomous Control Logic

Dynamic Decision Matrix

Our control system is based on a Decision Matrix that dictates how the robot reacts to sensory input. Instead of pre-programmed paths, the robot makes real-time choices. This logic is categorized into three distinct "Zones of Engagement":

  • Zone 1 (Safety Zone): If the distance is less than 15cm, the robot initiates an immediate motor brake to prevent collision.
  • Zone 2 (Tracking Zone): If the distance is between 20cm and 50cm, the robot maintains a forward velocity to stay with the user.
  • Zone 3 (Idle Zone): If the distance is greater than 100cm, the robot stops and scans, assuming the target is lost.

This conditional logic ensures the robot behaves in a way that feels natural and responsive to the user's movements.

SLIDE 07 / 20

‚ö° L298N Dual H-Bridge Driver

Power Regulation and Distribution

Microcontrollers like the Arduino lack the current capacity to drive high-torque DC motors. Therefore, we integrated the L298N Dual H-Bridge Motor Driver. This module acts as an electrical gateway. It takes low-power commands from the Arduino pins and translates them into high-power currents from our 9V battery bank.

The H-Bridge circuit is essential because it allows the robot to change the direction of current flow to the motors. This gives us the ability to move forward, move backward, or turn by spinning wheels in opposite directions (differential steering).

üìå The Technical Pinout Diagram

Input and Output (I/O) Management

Managing the Pinout Configuration is a vital step in hardware integration. Every sensor and actuator requires a specific dedicated pin on the Arduino. For this project, we designated specific digital pins for the Trigger and Echo functions of the sonar, and dedicated PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) pins for motor speed control.

PWM is a technique used to control the speed of the motors by switching the power on and off very quickly. This allows Maier and the team to make the robot move slowly when it is close to the user and speed up when the user starts to walk away.

üîã Power Supply and Battery Management

Energy Storage and Distribution

Robotics projects face a major challenge known as "Electrical Noise." When motors spin, they create small spikes in electricity that can interfere with the Arduino‚Äôs delicate circuitry. To solve this, Team BETA utilized a Dual-Power Strategy. We used a 9V battery to power the motors through the L298N driver and a separate regulated supply for the Arduino logic.

Ensuring that the robot has enough "Current Capacity" is the difference between a robot that moves and a robot that just makes a humming sound. Our battery system provides the necessary Amps to keep the robot moving for the duration of the presentation.

üìè Microsecond Precision

Software Timing and Logic

The Arduino does not measure distance in inches; it measures time in Microseconds. One microsecond is one-millionth of a second. To achieve a smooth "follow" effect, our code must process these time values extremely quickly. Maier programmed the system to ignore "False Positives"‚Äîspikes in sound that aren't actually the target‚Äîensuring the robot doesn't jitter or move randomly.

üèóÔ∏è Mechanical Chassis Design

Structural Integrity and Balance

The physical frame, or Chassis, was engineered for balance. If the robot is too top-heavy, the sudden acceleration from the motors would cause it to flip over. We placed the heavy batteries at the lowest point of the frame to create a low center of gravity. This mechanical advantage allows the robot to start and stop abruptly while remaining perfectly stable.

üõ†Ô∏è Hardware Debugging Procedures

Solving Real-World Technical Issues

No project is perfect on the first try. Team BETA encountered "Phase Inversion" where the motors were spinning in opposite directions, causing the robot to spin in circles. Through systematic Debugging, we traced the issue to a mirrored connection on the L298N board. Correcting this required a deep understanding of H-Bridge logic and motor polarity.
